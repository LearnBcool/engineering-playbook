# Engineering Conversation Protocol (ECP-001)

> Project Zero Protocol
> Foundation v1

---

# Protocol Information

**Protocol ID**

ECP-001

---

**Protocol Name**

Project Zero

---

**Related Stage**

Stage 00 — Project Zero

---

# Objective

Transform an initial project idea into a clearly understood engineering problem.

The goal is to establish alignment before any technical work begins.

No implementation decisions are made during this protocol.

---

# Trigger

Start automatically when:

- A new project is proposed.
- A major initiative begins.
- An existing project requires complete redefinition.

---

# Participants

## Engineering Leader

Responsible for leading the meeting, making final decisions, and maintaining focus.

---

## Problem Analyst

Responsible for understanding the real problem behind the proposed solution.

Questions assumptions.

Clarifies objectives.

---

## Research Engineer

Responsible for identifying knowledge gaps, comparable solutions, and research opportunities.

---

## Leadership Observer

Observes the meeting without influencing technical decisions.

Evaluates:

- Leadership
- Communication
- Participation
- Decision quality

Provides feedback only after the meeting ends.

---

# Facilitator

Engineering Leader

---

# Decision Authority

Engineering Leader

The objective is consensus whenever possible.

When consensus cannot be reached, the Engineering Leader makes the final decision.

---

# Meeting Rules

- Understand before proposing solutions.
- Challenge assumptions respectfully.
- One speaker at a time.
- Support opinions with reasoning.
- Record every important decision.
- Avoid technology discussions.
- Stay focused on the engineering objective.

---

# Discussion Flow

## Phase 1 — Project Vision

Leader presents the project idea.

Expected Output:

Project Vision

---

## Phase 2 — Problem Analysis

Problem Analyst leads the discussion.

Questions:

- What problem exists?
- Who experiences it?
- Why does it matter?

Expected Output:

Problem Statement

---

## Phase 3 — Stakeholders

Identify everyone involved.

Expected Output:

Stakeholder Register

---

## Phase 4 — Constraints

Identify:

- Budget
- Time
- Technical
- Legal
- Team

Expected Output:

Constraint Register

---

## Phase 5 — Assumptions

List assumptions.

Classify each as:

- Validated
- Unvalidated
- High Risk

Expected Output:

Assumption Register

---

## Phase 6 — Risks

Identify initial engineering risks.

Expected Output:

Risk Register

---

## Phase 7 — Success

Define measurable success.

Expected Output:

Success Metrics

---

## Phase 8 — Open Questions

Document unanswered questions.

Every question becomes a future action.

Expected Output:

Open Questions Register

---

## Phase 9 — Engineering Decision

Engineering Leader summarizes.

Possible outcomes:

- GO
- NO-GO
- RESEARCH REQUIRED

Expected Output:

Engineering Decision Record (EDR)

---

# Conflict Resolution

If disagreement occurs:

1. Clarify assumptions.
2. Review available evidence.
3. Identify missing information.
4. Request additional research if necessary.
5. Engineering Leader makes the final decision.

Disagreement is expected.

Unresolved disagreement must be documented.

---

# Outputs

This protocol must produce:

- Project Vision
- Problem Statement
- Stakeholder Register
- Constraint Register
- Assumption Register
- Risk Register
- Success Metrics
- Open Questions Register
- Engineering Decision Record (EDR)

---

# Completion Criteria

The protocol is complete only when:

- Every discussion phase has finished.
- Every required output exists.
- Engineering decisions are documented.
- The Engineering Leader formally closes the meeting.

---

# Leadership Reflection

After the meeting, the Leadership Observer evaluates:

- Was the objective achieved?
- Was everyone heard?
- Were assumptions challenged?
- Were decisions evidence-based?
- Was the discussion focused?
- What can improve in the next meeting?

Leadership feedback is documented separately from engineering decisions.

---

# Engineering Principle

The quality of a project depends on the quality of its first conversation.

Project Zero exists to ensure that every project begins with clarity, alignment, and shared understanding.
